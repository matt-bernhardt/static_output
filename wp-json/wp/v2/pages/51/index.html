As technology changes, researchers should plan for both hardware and software obsolescence and consider the longevity of their file format choices to ensure long term readability and access.<\/p>\n

File formats more likely to be accessible in the future have the following characteristics:<\/p>\n

    \n
  • Non-proprietary<\/li>\n
  • Open, documented standard<\/li>\n
  • Common usage by research community<\/li>\n
  • Standard representation (ASCII, Unicode)<\/li>\n
  • Unencrypted<\/li>\n
  • Uncompressed<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

    Examples of preferred file format choices include:<\/p>\n

      \n
    • ODF or LaTeX or TXT, not Word<\/li>\n
    • ASCII, not Excel<\/li>\n
    • MPEG-4, not Quicktime<\/li>\n
    • TIFF or JPEG2000, not GIF or JPG<\/li>\n
    • XML or RDF, not RDBMS<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

      Consider migrating your data into a format with the above characteristics, in addition to keeping a copy in the original software format. Note, in some cases, migrating data to an open format may cause data\/metadata loss.<\/p>\n

      See also\u00a0Tools for Understanding Digital Files (web page)<\/a>.<\/p>\n

      If you deposit your data in a\u00a0repository<\/a>, your files may be migrated\u00a0to newer formats, so that they’re usable to future researchers.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
